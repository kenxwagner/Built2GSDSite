About Company

If you think there are ways to bring ideas to life, name them. Most of the advertisements for "Inventor's Wanted" belong to predatory scam companies’ intent on manipulation of a person's ignorance of the patent process. Another chunk of invention lookers belongs to patent troll companies.

There are two main schools of thought for any would be "Edison". The idea pushed most often is to take your best idea and to spend time and money on getting a patent, marketing it, prototyping, etc. And if you are lucky, you might see a chance of success from the thousands of dollars you spent on it. Stats thrown around carelessly are commonly claiming a 2-3% success rate for this method. I tried that for years and found it frustrating (especially with no money). Lo and behold, a few books and dozens of websites later I find the book From Patent to Profit and things start clicking. This has book lead me to the other school of thought (the one this site is based on). This wild idea is to network with people who have connections (manufacturing, etc.) and experience in the area of the idea. To back it up, look at Eli Whitney, Thomas Edison, Alexander Graham Bell, George Washington Carver, etc. None of them invented "One" great idea, they did far more. This second method also answers a problem I had for years with the other method. Namely, if I have lots of ideas, how do I know which one is the best one?

Problem solved!

History

Since I was little, I've had a unquenchable thirst for knowledge and an unstoppable curiosity for how everything works. I first tried to pursue an invention when I was a teenager. I never sent anything off to the company I contacted. I entered college with the goal of being a creative force in the world of Engineering. More specifically, I entered into Mechanical Engineering at WSU in Pullman, WA. Much to my dismay, I learned that my curiosity, mechanical aptitude, and hands on approach to designing and building were in the wrong place. I could find few people calling themselves engineers that worked on their own cars and like to build things or take items apart to discover their inner workings. I was truly shocked to learn that the majority of companies have you sign agreements that sign over all intellectual property you create while working there. The other part I found annoying was that one doesn't get to work anything hands on until the Junior and Senior level. After 2 years I went into computer science in order to make money doing something I already had ability to do.

To back track a little.... I was 20 (second year at college) and submitted a very good idea to Invention Submission Corporation and got the whole "It's the greatest thing since sliced bread!" routine. I spent $688 for the next step, but fortunately failed to gather the $10,000  (during my 3rd year in college, but at Columbia Basin College in Pasco, WA) asked to paid for the final step. I would have still been paying that back to this day.

I have devoured books, websites, and insites on inventing, patenting, etc. The best book I've had was written by Bob Demattis called Patent to Profit.  From his suggestions I got so much further than I had previously. I found out that the idea of not saying a peep to anyone is moronic. I spoke to engineers (of many disciplines), doctors, dentists, teachers, consumers, and anyone interested in giving their opinion on an idea. Some of the ideas were great-I probably discussed over 100 different patentable ideas while living in the Tri-Cites area, but none of them had any connections to further the idea. I even met some patent holders. I kept at it, though I was at the same time trying to move to a larger city with more resources. My best advice came from Mr. Henderson of Nuvotec (they specialize in dealing with nuclear waste) when I asked if I should try in the Portland area, to which he replied, "Yes, that would be a much better place." I tried to transfer through Home Depot, Lowe's, Ferguson, and finally got it with Fred Meyer (it was part-time mind you!). I met with poor managment at thoses locations and found out some people without ethics sometimes have management titles.

I moved to Hillsboro, OR on September 1, 2005. A few months of searching got me connected to the newly formed Portland Inventors Group. Though, I did not find too much help in this group (still outstanding people). Many of them are part of the one-idea-and-lots-of-time-and-money school of thought. I am of the network first school of thought. When I mentioned the idea of a website for inventors by inventors, I found some liked it, but no one was interested in making it. I held off on it until I found www.DreamstoMarket.com and got inspired. At first I tried to contact the site owner, but the e-mails went unanswered. Further study found the flaws of having open membership. I concluded that the site needs to be local only to keep costs much lower. Also, it should have a membership set up so that only those that have been checked are allowed in. I've had enough run-ins with "inventor companies" (both scam artist and patent trolls).

With Oregon experiencing one of it's worst economic times is decades, I moved back to the Tri-Cities area. What you see is a work in progress, as it should be.

Ken Wagner

Mission Statement

    To promote inventions and intellectual property using the best tools around. Memberships only to reduce the risk of scams and unethical practices. A simple networking method that is created to spark another industrial revolution. Yeah, I dream big.
